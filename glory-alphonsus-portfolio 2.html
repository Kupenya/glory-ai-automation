About Me

The engineer
who crossed over.

I spent two years in the backend — designing APIs, thinking about database schemas at odd hours, arguing about authentication flows, and learning exactly where systems break under pressure. That foundation isn't a detour. It's an unfair advantage.

"Most automation engineers don't understand systems. Most backend engineers won't touch automation. I decided to be both."

When I moved into AI automation, I brought everything with me: security-first thinking, an understanding of rate limits and error handling, and the habit of asking "what happens when this breaks?" before shipping. My automations aren't just fast — they're resilient.

I use Make, n8n, and Airtable as my primary toolkit, and I'm comfortable reaching into the underlying APIs when the no-code layer isn't enough. Which, in my experience, is often.

▸ Personal Motivation
What excites you most about AI automation?
The leverage. One well-built automation can multiply someone's capacity 10x. I'm excited that we're at a point where intelligence can be embedded directly into workflows — not just speed, but genuine reasoning. That's a massive unlock, and I want to be the person building those systems.
▸ Problem-Solving Approach
How do you approach a new automation challenge?
I start with the failure modes, not the happy path. Two years of backend work trained me to ask: what breaks first? Then I map the data flow, identify trust boundaries, and design with error handling baked in — not bolted on at the end.
▸ Career Goals
Where are you headed?
I want to be the engineer who bridges AI capability and business reality — building systems that are intelligent and production-grade. In 2–3 years, I see myself leading automation strategy somewhere that genuinely moves the business forward with this work.
